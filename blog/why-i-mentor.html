Why I Mentor — and Why You Should Too

Mentoring isn't just about giving back — it's one of the fastest ways to grow your own skills, perspective, and impact.

I started mentoring because someone asked. I continue because it's made me better at everything I do.

When I first began mentoring IT professionals, I thought I was offering my time and experience. And I was. But what I didn't expect was how much I would gain in return. Here's what mentoring has taught me — and why I believe every experienced professional should do it.

Mentoring Forces Clarity

There's a difference between knowing something and being able to explain it. When someone asks you why you made a decision or how you approached a problem, you can't hide behind vague answers. You have to articulate your thinking clearly.

This has made me a better communicator, a better leader, and a better builder. When I'm designing a tool now, I often imagine explaining it to someone I'm mentoring — and that perspective catches design flaws before I write a line of code.

Mentoring Builds Perspective

IT can be insular. We get deep into our own systems, processes, and challenges. Mentoring someone from a different team, company, or background forces you to step outside that bubble.

I've had mentees ask me questions that made me realize I'd been doing something a certain way for years without questioning it. The fresh perspective of someone new to the field is incredibly valuable. They see the assumptions you've stopped noticing.

Mentoring Creates Leverage

As an individual contributor, your impact is limited to what you can do yourself. As a leader, your impact grows through your team. As a mentor, your impact multiplies — because the people you mentor go on to mentor others.

I've seen junior engineers I mentored become team leads, then managers, then mentors themselves. The knowledge and confidence I shared with them has now propagated to dozens of people I've never met. That's leverage that no individual achievement can match.

How to Start Mentoring

If you're reading this and thinking "I'd like to mentor but I'm not sure I'm ready" — you are. Here's how to start:

  • Start with one person. A colleague who's junior to you, a student, someone from a professional network. One conversation is enough to begin.
  • Listen more than you talk. Good mentoring isn't about giving advice. It's about asking questions that help the other person find their own answers.
  • Be specific. Instead of "let me know if you need help," offer something concrete: "I can review your resume" or "Let's look at that problem together for 30 minutes."
  • Use a platform. I use Calendly, but LinkedIn, industry communities, or even your own company's internal networks are great places to start.

The ROI of Mentoring

Every hour I spend mentoring comes back to me multiplied — in clarity, perspective, relationships, and impact. If you have experience that others could benefit from, don't wait until you feel ready. Someone out there needs exactly what you know.

Ready to start? Book a session with me — or better yet, find someone to mentor today. You'll both be better for it.
