How I Mentor IT Teams to Embrace AI Without the Hype

A practical framework for introducing AI to IT teams — no buzzwords, no fear, just real workflows and measurable wins.

Every IT leader I talk to these days has the same question: "How do I get my team to actually use AI without it being a distraction?"

It's a fair concern. The market is flooded with tools claiming to revolutionize everything. Team members either dismiss it as hype or jump in without a plan. Neither approach works.

Over the past year, I've mentored several IT teams through this transition. Here's the framework I use — it's simple, practical, and focuses on building momentum through small wins.

Step 1: Start with Their Pain, Not the Technology

The biggest mistake I see is leading with the tool: "Let's use Copilot!" or "We need to adopt AI." That's backwards. Start with the team's actual frustrations:

  • What tasks do they dread every week?
  • Where do they spend time on things that feel repetitive?
  • What information is hard to find even though it exists somewhere?

When you start with pain points, AI becomes a solution to a problem they already have — not an abstract initiative from leadership. The conversation shifts from "we have to learn this" to "this actually helps me."

Step 2: One Tool, One Workflow, One Week

I ask teams to pick one repetitive task and one AI tool, and commit to trying it for one week. Not a pilot program with KPIs and steering committees. Just a practical experiment.

Some examples I've seen work:

  • Using ChatGPT to draft weekly status reports from bullet points
  • Using Copilot to generate boilerplate code for common IT tasks
  • Using Perplexity for faster root cause research during incidents

The one-week constraint removes the pressure to be perfect. It's an experiment, not a deployment. Most teams find at least one workflow where the tool genuinely saves time — and that's the hook.

Step 3: Build a Simple Sharing Habit

The fastest way to spread adoption is peer-to-peer. I encourage teams to start a simple channel — Slack, Teams, or even a shared doc — where people post one thing each week: "I used AI for X and it saved Y time."

No presentations. No formal training. Just real examples from real team members. When people see a colleague saving two hours on report writing, they naturally want to try it themselves.

This organic spread is far more effective than top-down mandates. I've seen teams go from zero adoption to daily AI usage in under a month, just through shared examples.

Step 4: Address the Fears Directly

Every IT team has legitimate concerns about AI. I've found it's better to address them openly than to brush them aside:

  • "AI will replace my job." — It won't. But someone who uses AI well might replace someone who doesn't. The goal is augmentation, not automation of your role.
  • "I don't trust the output." — Good. You shouldn't blindly trust it. Treat AI like a junior team member — it drafts, you review. The responsibility stays with you.
  • "I don't know where to start." — That's what Step 1 is for. Pick one small task. Try it for a week.

Mentoring through these concerns is where the real value lies. It's not about teaching people how to use ChatGPT. It's about helping them develop a mindset where they can evaluate any AI tool critically and practically.

The Result

The teams I've worked with using this framework don't just adopt AI — they become self-sufficient. They start identifying their own opportunities, experimenting with new tools, and sharing what works.

And that's the real goal of mentoring: not to give answers, but to build the capability to find them.
