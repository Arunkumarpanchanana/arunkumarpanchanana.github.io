How an IT Leader Discovered a Passion for Building AI Tools

How 15+ years in IT service management led me to building AI tools on the side — and why the two aren't as different as you'd think.

If you'd told me five years ago that I'd be building AI tools, I wouldn't have believed you.

My career was in IT service management — the world of SLAs, incident management, change control, and keeping enterprise systems running. It's a world where predictability is prized and "innovation" usually means upgrading to the next version of a ticketing system.

But here's what I learned: building reliable IT systems and building AI tools are closer than they look. Both are about understanding a problem deeply, designing a solution that fits, and iterating until it works.

The IT Leader Years

For over 15 years, I worked across IT operations and service management. I led teams responsible for keeping critical systems available, managing major incidents, and delivering complex projects. It taught me:

  • Process thinking — how to break chaotic operations into repeatable workflows
  • Root cause analysis — the discipline of finding the real problem, not the surface symptom
  • Communication — translating between technical teams and business stakeholders
  • Delivery under pressure — keeping things moving when everything is on fire

These skills turned out to be more valuable for building AI tools than any programming course. Why? Because most "AI projects" fail not due to technical limitations, but due to poor problem definition, unclear requirements, and lack of operational thinking.

The Pivot

The turning point came when I started experimenting with automation. I noticed that many of the repetitive tasks my teams handled could be streamlined — not with expensive enterprise software, but with simple scripts and workflows. I started building small internal tools to solve specific pain points.

One tool led to another. Before I knew it, I was building applications that used AI to solve problems I'd wrestled with for years: matching resumes to job descriptions, generating daily learning content, and automating routine decisions.

The shift wasn't from "IT guy" to "developer." It was from "someone who manages systems" to "someone who builds solutions." The domain knowledge from years in IT gave me an unfair advantage — I knew exactly what problems were worth solving.

What I Wish I Knew Sooner

Looking back, a few things stand out:

  • You don't need to be a software engineer. Modern AI tools and APIs make it possible to build useful applications without a CS degree. Start with what you know.
  • Your domain expertise is your superpower. The hardest part of building tools is knowing what to build. Years of IT experience taught me exactly where the pain points are.
  • Shipping is everything. A simple tool that works today is worth more than a perfect tool that ships next quarter.

Where I Am Now

Today, I split my time between three things: leading IT teams, building AI tools, and mentoring others who want to make a similar shift. Each role feeds the others. The IT experience keeps my tools grounded in reality. The tool building gives me fresh perspectives on old problems. And mentoring forces me to articulate what I've learned — which is the best way to learn it yourself.

If you're in IT and curious about AI, my advice is simple: start with a problem you already have. Not with a technology. Not with a trend. Just one thing that frustrates you about your daily work, and see if you can build something to fix it.

That's how every tool on this site started.
